Scientists Achieve First Quantum Teleportation Between Independent Light Sources — A Critical Step Toward the Quantum Internet

An international team spanning Germany, Italy, and beyond has transmitted quantum information across a 270-meter free-space link, proving the viability of a technology that could one day make communications unhackable

Editor P-A Gustafsson December 7, 2025 · 9 min read

Rome — In a dimly lit laboratory at Sapienza University, a photon carrying a fragile quantum state vanished from one corner of a sprawling experimental apparatus. Two hundred seventy meters away, across fiber cables and an open-air optical link that stretched between university buildings, an identical twin emerged — bearing the precise quantum signature of its distant sibling. The original photon's information had been destroyed in the process, but its essence had traveled instantaneously, defying the constraints of ordinary physics.

This is quantum teleportation, and according to research published this week in Nature Communications, an international team has now demonstrated it can work between two completely independent light sources — a milestone that physicists have pursued for more than a decade.

The breakthrough, achieved by researchers from Paderborn University in Germany, Sapienza University of Rome, the University of Stuttgart, Saarland University, and the Leibniz Institute for Solid State and Materials Research in Dresden, represents what one researcher called "the first practical step toward building quantum repeaters that could one day span continents."

Why This Matters

Unlike classical information, which can be copied and verified, quantum information follows stranger rules. Measure it and you destroy it. Copy it and the laws of physics rebel. These constraints, which seem like obstacles, are precisely what make quantum communication attractive: any attempt to intercept a quantum message leaves an indelible trace.

But there's a problem. Quantum information degrades over distance. Photons carrying quantum states get absorbed by fiber optic cables, scattered by the atmosphere, lost to the inevitable entropy of physical systems. "You can send a quantum state perhaps a hundred kilometers through fiber before losses become prohibitive," explained Dr. Klaus Jöns of Paderborn University, a lead author on the study. "To go further, you need repeaters — nodes that can catch incoming quantum information and pass it along without destroying its quantum properties."

That's where this experiment comes in. The researchers demonstrated teleportation between two independent quantum dots — tiny semiconductor structures that emit single photons on demand. Previous experiments had achieved teleportation using a single source or entangled particles originating from the same location. This is different: two separate quantum emitters, fabricated independently, producing photons that had never interacted until the moment of teleportation.

"What we have shown is that devices produced in different laboratories, with different fabrication processes, can still interface well enough to teleport quantum states. This is essential for any realistic quantum network, where you cannot expect all nodes to be built identically." — Dr. Klaus Jöns, Paderborn University

The Technical Achievement

Making two independent quantum dots "speak the same language" is extraordinarily difficult. Quantum dots are not identical; each one emits photons at slightly different wavelengths, with different timing characteristics, affected by imperfections at the atomic scale. To achieve teleportation, the photons from both sources must be indistinguishable — identical enough that the universe itself cannot tell them apart.

The team accomplished this through a combination of techniques: embedding their gallium arsenide quantum dots in nanophotonic cavities to control their emission, applying magnetic fields to tune the wavelengths, and using ultrafast superconducting nanowire detectors that could filter out imperfectly matched photons after they had interacted.

The result: 82% fidelity in teleporting the polarization state of photons from one quantum dot to another. This exceeded the classical limit — the best performance achievable without genuine quantum effects — by more than ten standard deviations. In physics, that level of certainty borders on mathematical proof.

The Road to a Quantum Internet

The implications extend far beyond laboratory demonstrations. Government intelligence agencies around the world have quietly acknowledged that quantum computers, when they become sufficiently powerful, will be able to break most of the encryption that currently protects global communications. Banks, militaries, diplomatic cables, medical records — all are secured by mathematical problems that quantum computers may one day solve in seconds.

A quantum internet offers a way out. Because its security is guaranteed by the laws of physics rather than mathematical complexity, it would remain secure even against adversaries with unlimited computational power. China has already launched quantum communication satellites and built quantum-secured networks spanning thousands of kilometers. The European Union's EuroQCI initiative aims to create a continent-wide quantum communication infrastructure by decade's end.

But none of these efforts have solved the repeater problem. Current quantum networks are limited to point-to-point links or require trusted relay nodes that reintroduce vulnerability. What this Rome experiment demonstrates is that the building blocks for truly trustless quantum repeaters — repeaters that never need to decode the quantum information they handle — may finally be within reach.

The International Race

The publication of this research comes at an emblematic moment. UNESCO has designated 2025 the International Year of Quantum Science and Technology, and nations from the United States to China to Germany are investing billions in quantum infrastructure. Last week, the UK and Germany announced £14 million in new joint funding for quantum research and standardization. China unveiled its UnitaryLab 1.0 platform, claiming computational speedups of potentially a trillion times for complex equations.

The Rome experiment — conducted across national boundaries, with researchers from five institutions in three countries — reflects a field where international collaboration remains essential even as geopolitical tensions rise. Quantum physics does not respect borders, and neither does the community working to harness it.

For ordinary citizens of any nation, the stakes are both abstract and intensely personal. The outcome of this research — and the race to build quantum networks — will determine whether the private conversations, financial transactions, and medical records of the next generation remain private, or become readable to anyone with sufficiently advanced technology.

In a university building in Rome, two photons just suggested that privacy might yet have a future.
